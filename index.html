CONCEPT 01 — PTB

Pre-Trip Comms Briefing Service

"The thing every travel security firm should offer but doesn't — a field-ready, country-specific intelligence package covering exactly what mobile network to use, what to avoid, and how to configure your devices before you land."

The Concept

The travel security industry — Control Risks, Kroll, Healix, Drum Cussac — delivers pre-trip briefings covering political risk, physical threat environments, medical infrastructure, and evacuation protocols. What none of them deliver, and what every journalist, corporate due diligence team, NGO field worker, and PE associate doing an emerging market site visit actually needs, is a communications terrain briefing: an actionable, field-ready document that answers exactly how to stay connected, stay private, and avoid the invisible risks of mobile networks in a specific country.

Coverage maps lie. Carrier marketing says nothing about surveillance posture, infrastructure reliability during political events, or the practical reality of which operator is fastest in the neighborhoods your client is actually visiting. A Pre-Trip Comms Briefing fills this gap with a structured deliverable: the best local operators with honest assessments of their quality and government relationships, SIM acquisition logistics (airport vs. local store, registration requirements and what data they expose), recommended device configuration, known network threats and IMSI catcher activity in that country, and practical do/don't guidance calibrated to the client's risk profile.

This is the fastest path to revenue of any concept in this document. The deliverable is intelligence — your inventory is knowledge you already possess. A PDF template, a clean website, and five sample briefs for high-demand destinations are sufficient to begin selling. No infrastructure. No capital-intensive build. No regulatory filings. The barrier to entry is being the person who actually knows this — which you are and your competitors are not.

The market for travel security briefings already exists and is well-funded. You are not creating demand. You are inserting yourself into an established buying process with a product that should already be there but isn't.

The Gap

What Doesn't Exist

Travel risk firms brief physical and medical threats. Corporate IT gives generic VPN advice. Telecom carriers give you a roaming rate card. Travel blogs give you "get an Airalo eSIM." None of these tell you that Safaricom in Kenya is excellent in Nairobi but unreliable in the Northern Corridor, or that the dominant operator in Belarus shares subscriber data with state security services in real time, or that physical SIMs in Azerbaijan require biometric registration that creates a permanent government record of your presence.

This information exists in the heads of people who have spent careers inside the global telecoms system. It does not exist in any consumable, packaged product. The market has no Jane's for mobile networks. You can be that product.

CONCEPT 02 — MTI

Mobile Terrain Intelligence

"The first subscription intelligence service that treats the global mobile network landscape the way Stratfor treats geopolitics — analytical, opinionated, and useful to people who need to operate in it."

The Concept

Mobile Terrain Intelligence is the strategic, long-game concept in this document — the one that builds the deepest brand authority, creates the most durable recurring revenue, and over time positions you as the definitive analytical voice on the global mobile network landscape. It is also the concept that requires the most patience, because audience-built businesses compound slowly before they compound fast.

The product is a subscription intelligence publication covering the global mobile network landscape as a serious analytical domain: which operators are gaining ground and why, when connectivity becomes a geopolitical instrument, what infrastructure investments signal about a government's strategic priorities, how network degradation correlates with political instability, where the eSIM narrative obscures real market dynamics. Published on a regular cadence — weekly free edition, deeper paid editions — and positioned for the cohort of professionals who operate in the intersection of technology, geopolitics, and field work.

The gap this fills is absolute: there is no publication, no newsletter, no research service that covers mobile networks as geopolitical and operational intelligence. The trade press (Light Reading, Fierce Wireless, Telecom TV) covers the industry for industry insiders. Nobody covers it for the people who have to make decisions based on what those networks actually do in the field.

You're not competing with eSIM apps. You're competing with Stratfor, The Interpreter, and Lawfare — publications that turned deep domain expertise into sustainable subscription businesses. The model is proven; the domain is vacant.

MTI is also the flywheel that feeds every other concept in this document. A well-read publication creates credibility for Pre-Trip Briefings. It creates a list of warm leads for OPSEC Advisory engagements. It creates the data and case study inventory that powers Network Posture Monitoring. It creates the audience for the Physical SIM Platform. Building MTI is building the infrastructure for a portfolio of related businesses.

The Gap

What the Market is Missing

The mobile network landscape is one of the most consequential and least understood geopolitical domains. Governments use networks to suppress dissent. Network outages predict political instability. Infrastructure investment patterns reveal strategic priorities before official announcements. Surveillance posture varies dramatically by country. eSIM policy is a proxy war for national sovereignty. None of this is covered anywhere that matters.

The professionals who most need this intelligence — journalists, political risk analysts, investors with emerging market exposure, NGO security directors, corporate travelers, and the growing cohort of intelligence-aware travelers — have no dedicated source. They piece together fragments from Reddit, telecom trade press, anecdotes from colleagues, and outdated blog posts. A publication that aggregates, synthesizes, and adds real analytical value commands genuine attention and real money.

Communications OPSEC Advisory

"A boutique advisory that does for mobile communications what Control Risks does for physical security — trains organizations to understand and navigate the specific radio-layer threats that put their people at risk in the field."

The Concept

The communications OPSEC gap is hiding in plain sight. The corporate security industry runs extensive risk management programs for organizations that send people into dangerous environments — physical threat assessments, kidnapping response protocols, medical evacuation plans. Journalists are briefed on hostile environment training. NGO workers complete security management courses. What none of these programs meaningfully addresses is the radio-layer threat: IMSI catchers in airports and government buildings, surveillance networks that log every device that connects to them, state-controlled operators that provide real-time location data to security services, and the specific ways that a standard smartphone with a standard SIM card can betray a traveler's identity, location, contacts, and movements before they've said a single word.

This is not abstract risk. Turkey, Russia, China, UAE, Egypt, Ethiopia, Pakistan, Belarus, and dozens of other countries operate infrastructure specifically designed to intercept and record mobile communications, identify foreign devices, and log user activity. A journalist entering one of these countries without a proper comms OPSEC protocol is not just inconvenienced — they are potentially putting sources, colleagues, and themselves at risk.

A Communications OPSEC Advisory fills this gap with three products: organizational assessments (auditing an organization's current mobile security posture and producing recommendations), training programs (half-day or full-day workshops that give field teams practical skills), and ongoing advisory retainers (available on-call as organizations deploy into new environments). The clients already exist and are already spending money on security — they're simply spending it on everything except this.

The posture shift you're making here: you are not a cybersecurity firm. You are a mobile network intelligence expert with deep operational knowledge of how networks are actually used as instruments of state power. That is a more defensible and more credible position than "cybersecurity consultant."

Legal and Investigative Firms: Law firms with international arbitration practices, forensic accounting firms, and due diligence specialists who conduct sensitive investigations involving hostile counterparties.

CONCEPT 04 — NPM

Network Posture Monitoring

"A continuous monitoring service that watches mobile network conditions in markets where organizations have operational exposure — producing alerts when network behavior signals risk before other intelligence sources do."

Viability
Capital to Launch $10,000 – $35,000
Time to First Revenue 3 – 6 months
Revenue Type Recurring / SaaS-like
Partner Urgency CRITICAL — Technical Build
The Concept

Political risk monitoring services — Verisk Maplecroft, Control Risks, Oxford Analytica — track news events, government actions, and macroeconomic indicators. None of them monitor the network layer. But the mobile network is one of the most sensitive leading indicators of political instability and state action available. Selective internet throttling precedes election fraud. Cell tower density changes predict military buildup. Network blackouts are the first tool of authoritarian crackdown — often deployed before official announcements reach international media. SIM registration policy changes signal shifting surveillance posture. Operator ownership changes that bring state influence into private infrastructure go largely unreported.

Network Posture Monitoring (NPM) is a subscription service that tracks these signals continuously in client-specified markets, synthesizes them into a network condition index, and sends alerts when conditions change in ways that indicate elevated risk. For a PE firm with a portfolio company in Indonesia, an alert that the dominant local operator has experienced unusual network throttling in the capital region — ahead of an election — is genuinely valuable leading intelligence. For an insurance underwriter with political risk exposure in Sub-Saharan Africa, a report that a government has changed SIM registration requirements in a way that indicates tightening social control is worth knowing before it becomes general news.

This is the highest-ceiling concept in this document, and also the most technically intensive. At launch, monitoring is manual — Nick and partner track signals, produce weekly reports, send alerts. As the client base grows, the monitoring layer is progressively automated. The long-term asset is a network condition database that becomes increasingly valuable as historical patterns validate the signal quality of the indicators.

The core bet is that network behavior is a leading indicator, not a lagging one. If that's true — and there's strong evidence it is — this is intelligence that political risk clients will pay recurring fees to receive.

What Gets Monitored

The Signal Set

Network Performance Anomalies: Unusual latency spikes, selective throttling by geography or application type, and bandwidth degradation that is inconsistent with network maintenance patterns. These are often the earliest signals of state intervention in network infrastructure.

Operator Ownership and Regulatory Changes: License modifications, ownership structure changes, government stake acquisition, and regulatory actions that change the effective control of network infrastructure.

SIM Policy and Registration Changes: New registration requirements, biometric collection mandates, and foreign SIM restrictions that indicate shifting surveillance or border control posture.

Infrastructure Events: New tower deployments, frequency allocation changes, and infrastructure investment patterns that signal strategic military or population control priorities.

Outage and Blackout Events: Selective or total network disruptions, with analysis of which networks are affected, in which geographies, at what times — cross-referenced against political and security events.

Target Market

Who Pays for This

Boutique PE Firms with EM Exposure: Firms like 57 Stars, Helios Investment Partners, and similar organizations with direct investments in frontier and emerging markets. A portfolio company's operational stability depends on the country's network infrastructure. Early warning of network disruption has direct P&L implications.

Political Risk Insurers: Underwriters at AXA XL, Lloyd's syndicates, and similar firms pricing political risk policies need leading indicators that current models don't include. Network posture data is a genuinely novel input.

Development Finance Institutions: IFC, OPIC (now DFC), CDC Group, and bilateral DFIs with project portfolios in complex markets. Their project monitoring requirements are extensive and compliance-driven.

Political Risk Consultancies: Firms like Oxford Analytica, Eurasia Group, and Teneo can license NPM as a data input to their own products, making them a channel partner rather than a direct competitor.

CONCEPT 05 — PSP

Physical SIM Intelligence Platform

"The definitive field guide to local SIM acquisition globally — an editorially opinionated, field-tested database built on the premise that the rush to eSIM has created a knowledge vacuum that matters to the people who travel seriously."

Viability

The Concept

The entire travel connectivity industry has unified around eSIM. Airalo, Holafly, Nomad, and dozens of competitors sell digital SIM profiles that travelers download before departure. The universal message is that eSIM is the future and physical SIMs are obsolete. This is demonstrably false in a significant portion of the world — and nobody is saying so clearly.

In Sub-Saharan Africa, across much of South and Southeast Asia, and in significant parts of Eastern Europe and Central Asia, local physical SIMs remain dramatically superior to eSIM alternatives: they're cheaper, faster, more reliable, and carry local numbers that matter for in-country communications. The eSIM products covering these markets are often running on inferior roaming agreements that produce materially worse speeds and reliability than simply buying a local SIM at the airport. The traveler who gets off a plane in Nairobi and activates an Airalo plan is often paying three times more for half the speed of an M-PESA Safaricom SIM they could have bought in the arrivals hall for the equivalent of four dollars.

The Physical SIM Intelligence Platform (PSP) is a database, editorial platform, and traveler resource that captures this knowledge: for every country in the world, what is the best local SIM option, where to acquire it, what the registration requirements mean for privacy and identity exposure, what the honest tradeoff is between eSIM convenience and local SIM quality, and what the surveillance posture of the dominant operators looks like. This is the platform Nick's personal knowledge has been building toward for twenty years — it is the anthropological project of tracking how cultures build their mobile connectivity infrastructure, expressed as a practical field guide.

Every market is different. Uzbekistan's Ucell vs. Beeline fight tells you something about Russian telecom influence in Central Asia. Safaricom's dominance in Kenya tells you something about where M-Pesa put its roots. This is not just connectivity advice — it is a window into how each country built its relationship with the mobile network era.

Why Now

The Moment

eSIM adoption has accelerated sharply since Apple launched eSIM-only iPhones in the US in 2022. The knowledge of how to navigate local SIM markets — which was previously common traveler knowledge — is rapidly being lost as a generation of travelers who never bought a physical SIM abroad grows up with eSIM as their default. The gap between "the eSIM marketing says this works" and "what actually works on the ground" is widening every year.

There is also a new urgency around privacy. Local SIM registration requirements have expanded dramatically across Asia, Africa, and the Middle East. Knowing what a registration requirement actually exposes — whether biometric data is collected, who has access to registration records, whether a tourist registration differs from a citizen registration — is genuinely valuable intelligence that no current product surfaces clearly.

Platform Architecture

What Gets Built

Country Guides: Structured profiles for every country covering the major operators, honest quality assessments, SIM acquisition logistics (airport, city center, documentation required), registration requirements and their privacy implications, and the honest eSIM vs. local SIM recommendation with context.

Field Notes: Shorter, travel-blog-style dispatches from specific cities or situations — "what actually happened when I tried to get a SIM in Ashgabat" or "the difference between Vodafone and local operators in Cairo." These are SEO-rich, shareable, and community-building.

Operator Intelligence: A secondary layer that covers operator ownership, government relationships, and surveillance posture for intelligence-aware travelers. This is where PSP overlaps with MTI content and creates a natural upsell path.

Community Layer: User-submitted signal reports — crowd-sourced speed tests, registration experience reports, acquisition difficulty scores — that keep data current and create engagement beyond editorial content.

"A managed connectivity service for professional services firms whose employees travel internationally — handling pre-trip provisioning, network optimization, and billing consolidation so that the client never has to think about it again."

The Concept

Enterprise mobility management is a mature market dominated by players like Verizon Business, Vodafone Global Enterprise, and Telnyx. Individual traveler eSIM is a mature market dominated by Airalo and its dozens of competitors. The gap between them — the professional services firm with 20–100 employees who travel internationally without meaningful support — remains largely unserved.

The International Connectivity Concierge fills this gap with a managed service: a dedicated connectivity partner for boutique professional services firms that handles everything their employees need to stay connected internationally. This means pre-trip eSIM provisioning with country-specific operator recommendations (not just "here's an Airalo link"), billing consolidation so the firm receives one monthly invoice instead of dozens of individual expense reports, annual connectivity audits to optimize spend, and direct access to an expert when something goes wrong at 11pm in Jakarta.

The intelligence layer is what separates this from a glorified eSIM reseller: the connectivity recommendations are backed by genuine network knowledge. When an associate is traveling to Uzbekistan for due diligence, the ICC doesn't just push them the cheapest eSIM — it tells them which operator to use and why, warns them about registration requirements that create data exposure, and recommends a backup satellite option if the meeting involves sensitive communications. That judgment is not something Airalo can replicate.

The clients this service targets — boutique PE firms, international law firms, development finance, and consulting boutiques — already spend substantially on travel. They are not price-sensitive. They are attention-sensitive. They will pay meaningfully for the service of never having to think about this again.

Target Market

Development Finance and Consulting: Firms like McKinsey's public sector practice, Dalberg, Palladium, and similar organizations with extensive field presence in emerging markets. They deploy people into complex environments regularly and have significant connectivity challenges that current solutions don't address.

Media Organizations with Field Correspondents: Regional news organizations, documentary production companies, and investigative journalism outlets with international production. The intelligence layer of the Concierge is especially valuable here.

What the Service Delivers

The Concierge Stack

Connectivity Profiling: At onboarding, every employee who travels internationally gets a connectivity profile — what devices they carry, where they travel regularly, what their usage patterns look like, and what their risk tolerance is for network security. Pre-Trip Provisioning: Before every international trip, the employee receives a provisioned eSIM with the ICC's recommended operator for that destination, a brief on what to expect, and backup options. Zero friction required from the traveler.

Billing Consolidation: All connectivity spend flows through one account, producing one monthly invoice to the firm. Expense reporting is eliminated. Spend tracking is automatic.

Expert Access: Direct line to Nick (or the team) for connectivity questions — in-trip troubleshooting, network advice, and real-time guidance for unusual situations.

Annual Optimization Review: Yearly analysis of the firm's connectivity spend, usage patterns, and network conditions in active markets, with recommendations for the coming year.
